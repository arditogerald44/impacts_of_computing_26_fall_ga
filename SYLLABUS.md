## 🗺️ 1. Course Description & Working Philosophy

### Institutional Course Description
This course considers the ethical and social impacts of contemporary technology with an emphasis on the impact of computing, especially on children (e.g., how modern-day information technologies affect our privacy, how social relations are affected, how ethical decisions will need to be made, etc.). Topics to be discussed include: ethics, social media, personal safety, reproductive and human enhancement technologies, information technologies, artificial intelligence, technology, and the environment.

### Our Core Philosophical Friction
Traditional tech frameworks operate on the working thesis that technology is a morally neutral tool that can be used well simply by understanding what it promotes or discourages. This course serves as a direct, critical investigation of that thesis. Drawing from **Ursula Franklin**, we analyze technology not as a neutral checklist of tools, but as an active, *prescriptive practice* that enforces political hierarchy, compliance, and social control. 

This repository operates entirely outside commercial, tracking-heavy Educational Technology (EdTech) spaces. We do not use proprietary Learning Management System dashboards for our weekly cycles. As **Audrey Watters** warns, traditional corporate learning dashboards function as Skinnerian teaching machines engineered to measure click telemetry and enforce behavioral compliance. 

Instead, we embrace **Minimal Computing**—a technical design philosophy that strips away software bloat, tracking cookies, and high-bandwidth dependencies to actively fight **Digital Redlining**. By working exclusively in plain-text Markdown via local Git terminal environments, we guarantee a learning space that is low-bandwidth, decentralized, deeply private, and accessible to anyone regardless of hardware access limitations.

To guide our technical audits, we adopt the cognitive split defined in the 2025 Vatican doctrinal note ***Antiqua et nova*** (§14). We recognize that computational networks excel at **Ratio** (discursive, mathematical optimization), but possess zero **Intellectus** (the intuitive, embodied human conscience). Throughout this term, your work will be an active defense of *intellectus* against the modern corporate urge to flatten human life into a black-box data matrix.

By the end of this semester, students will be able to:

### The Weekly Path Selection
Every week, you will choose **one** of two distinct operational tracks to complete your lab requirements. You may freely switch between them from week to week based on your technical goals:

* **Track A: The Developer Track:** Built for students pursuing hard algorithmic implementation and engineering diagnostics. Submissions require functional code execution scripts (`.py`, `.sh`, `.js`), dataset distribution verifications, and plain-text inline logic annotations.
* **Track B: The Analyst Track:** Built for students pursuing structural policy deconstruction, UX interface forensics, and socio-technical policy critique. Submissions require deeply structured analytical reports (`LAB-SUBMISSION.md`) checking live systems against our philosophical frameworks.

### 🧪 The Laboratory Sandbox Operational Registry
This repository contains four custom minimal-computing software simulators located in the `labs/` path directory. To complete your weekly lab push requests, you will run these localized interactive scripts to generate your own empirical raw tracking logs:
* `labs/02-nudge-simulation.html`: Simulates automated tracking triggers to evaluate Audrey Watters’ behaviorist compliance critiques.
* `labs/03-bias-simulation.html`: Generates data queues to demonstrate how micro-biasing vectors systematically evade macro-compliance checkers.
* `labs/04-surveillance-simulation.html`: Illustrates how tracking logic independently emerges from un-auditable, optimization-focused code.
* `labs/05-alchemy-simulation.html`: Runs scenario logic splits to verify Dreyfus's alchemical boundaries and category error critiques.

## ⚙️ 8. Repository Operation & Course Policies

### Code & Communication Etiquette

When communicating via repository issues or our secure Mattermost channels, maintaining a professional and respectful environment is mandatory.

- **Email Format:** When emailing your instructor, please use your Mville e-mail address and identify yourself by full name and course number. Place the course number, followed by the subject of the e-mail, in the subject line. Failure to adhere to these guidelines may result in a delayed response or no response at all.
- **Disagreement & Debate:** Classmate perspectives and opinions should be respected, even if they differ from one’s own positions. Disagreements, debates, and other contentious dialogue are welcomed, but must be carried out in a manner that focuses on finding better answers, not making personal attacks. Disruptive behavior will not be tolerated.

### Late Commit & Make-Up Policy

Because this is an asynchronous repository structure where peer evaluation and code reviews rely on timely submissions, extensions are not offered. Only coursework completed on time is eligible for full credit. Exceptions are processed exclusively under the following situations, provided that the instructor is notified in advance:

1. **Athletic / Sponsored Trips:** Travel dates accompanied by a signed memo from the coach must be presented to the instructor prior to travel dates.
2. **Religious Observances:** Advanced notice must be submitted to the instructor.
3. **Extenuating Circumstances:** Includes family emergencies, severe illnesses, or accidents. Students will work with SHAC to document such circumstances.
